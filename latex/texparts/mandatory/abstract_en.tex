This thesis assesses the state and effectiveness of contemporary social dialogue in the Czech Republic in a~European comparative perspective and identifies legal and organisational instruments transferable from selected European systems of social dialogue. The thesis hypothesis is: \emph{strengthening social dialogue and collective bargaining in the Czech Republic would contribute to sustainable economic development.} It is assessed through three lines of evidence: descriptive analysis documents weakening of social partners (declining trade-union and employer organisationdensity, low collective-bargaining coverage, very low active labour-market policy expenditure); comparison with the systems in Denmark, Austria, Germany, Poland and Slovakia places the Czech Republic among economies dominated by enterprise-level bargaining; and a~correlation analysis on the EU27 panel documents a~statistically significant relationship between collective-bargaining coverage, labour productivity and average actual working time. Four case studies of enterprise- and sector-level agreements then verify whether these findings are visible in the content of specific collective agreements. Building on these findings, the thesis formulates five concrete innovations of social dialogue: activation of the erga~omnes extension of sector-level collective agreements; partial delegation of active labour-market policy to trade unions following the Ghent model; pay transparency beyond the minimum transposition of EU Directive 2023/970; alignment of the status of solo self-employed and platform workers within collective bargaining; and a~public digital registry of collective agreements.
